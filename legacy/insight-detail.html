The Model Risk Playbook: What AI Teams Can Borrow from Financial Regulation

ML
Margaret Lin
Chief AI Officer, Rockmount AI
12 May 2025

Why SR 11-7 matters now

SR 11-7 — the Federal Reserve's 2011 supervisory guidance on model risk management — wasn't written for large language models. It was written for the credit scoring, market risk, and fraud detection models of the 2010s. But its core principles are more relevant today than they've ever been.

At its heart, SR 11-7 insists on three things: that models are subject to rigorous initial validation before deployment, that they're monitored continuously in production, and that the organisation maintains a clear inventory of what models it uses and for what purpose. If that sounds obvious, consider how few AI teams can actually claim all three.

The validation gap

Model validation — genuinely independent, documented testing before a system goes live — remains the single biggest gap in enterprise AI delivery. Most teams ship with internal testing, but internal testing is not validation. Validation requires a team or individual who was not involved in building the model to independently assess its performance, limitations, and failure modes.

The purpose of model validation is to verify that models are performing as intended and to identify model weaknesses and areas for improvement. — SR 11-7

Translating this to LLMs: validation should include output quality benchmarking on held-out test sets, adversarial probing for prompt injection and jailbreak susceptibility, demographic fairness analysis across all outputs that affect users differently by group, and latency and throughput testing under realistic production load profiles.

Model inventory: the foundation of governance

SR 11-7 requires institutions to maintain a "complete and current" inventory of all models in use. For most organisations now deploying AI, this inventory doesn't exist. Models proliferate through teams independently, often without central oversight, and frequently without documentation of what they're doing or who owns them.

A workable AI model inventory records: the model name and version, the vendor or internal team responsible, the primary use case, the data it processes (and whether it includes PII), the date of last validation, the outcome of that validation, and who has authority to modify or retrain the model.

It doesn't need to be complex. A well-maintained spreadsheet beats a sophisticated registry that nobody updates. The discipline matters more than the tooling.

Continuous monitoring in production

The third pillar of SR 11-7 is ongoing monitoring — the expectation that models are watched after deployment, not just at launch. For traditional ML, this usually means tracking accuracy or AUC on a holdout set and alerting when it degrades. For LLMs and agentic systems, it's more complex.

Effective LLM monitoring watches: output quality on golden-set test cases (run daily or weekly), refusal rate for sensitive query types (if applicable), latency and error rates by deployment region, and — where possible — downstream business outcomes that the model is meant to influence.

Making it practical for AI teams

The good news is that SR 11-7's principles are portable. You don't need a compliance department or a risk committee to implement them. Here's a minimum-viable governance stack for an AI product team:

  • A model card for each system, updated at every version bump. Model cards document purpose, data sources, performance, limitations, and human oversight provisions.
  • An independent validation checkpoint before every major release. "Independent" means someone who didn't write the code. A lateral review from another team is better than nothing.
  • A monitoring dashboard with at least three metrics tracked continuously: output quality, error rate, and a measure of distribution shift in inputs.
  • A monthly governance review where metrics are discussed by someone with authority to pause or roll back a model if needed.

None of this is heroic. It's the baseline that organisations using models for consequential decisions should already be doing. The question is whether the AI governance wave sweeping enterprise software will produce real change — or just new slide decks with frameworks nobody implements.
